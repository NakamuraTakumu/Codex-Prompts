# Paper Revision Principles

Use these principles as default constraints for manuscript revision.

## Core Principles

- Unless instructed otherwise, do not change meaning, claims, implications, or logical relations.
- Unless instructed otherwise, do not widen the edit to untouched passages or unrequested issues.
- If the problem is too large for a safe local fix, do not rewrite broadly on your own. Explain where the problem is and keep it as a finding unless the user expands the scope.
- Prefer local edits with smaller semantic drift before larger rewrites.
- When several revisions are possible, prefer the one with the smallest meaning change over the most polished phrasing.
- Match the manuscript's existing vocabulary, style, English level, and notation instead of introducing a different authorial voice.
- Keep terminology, symbols, definitions, abbreviations, reference expressions, and citation handling consistent with the rest of the paper.
- Even for local English edits, do not accidentally change claim strength, attribution, certainty, or limiting conditions.
- Treat citation revision as sentence-level and rhetoric-level revision, not as a macro substitution exercise.
- Do not add new claims, support, comparisons, examples, caveats, warnings, or interpretations unless requested.
- When revising a word or expression, make sure the change does not break its role in nearby context or elsewhere in the manuscript.
- Even when the source-level edit looks small, avoid making rendered PDF behavior worse around references, figure connections, line breaks, lists, or equations.

## Intended Scope

- Apply these principles to English phrasing fixes, citation integration, wording adjustments after style-file changes, local logical cleanup, and LaTeX readability improvements.
- Do not rely on these principles alone for major restructuring or revisions that intentionally change the paper's claims.
